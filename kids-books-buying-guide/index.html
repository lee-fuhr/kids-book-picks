How to choose a kids books

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choose kids' books by matching the format to your child's age and reading stage, picture books for preschoolers, early reader chapter books for emerging readers, then weigh factors like reading level, interest, illustration quality, and durability. Trust your child's engagement more than any label.

What age range should I look for in a picture book?

Picture books are typically aimed at children ages 2 to 7, but the intended audience varies by complexity. A simple board book with one object per page suits a toddler, while a storybook with multiple sentences per page and a gentle plot works for a four- or five-year-old. Many publishers print a suggested age range on the back cover or inside flap, use that as a starting point, not a rule. Keep in mind that a child’s attention span and comprehension matter more than a precise number. A three-year-old who loves long stories may sit through a book labeled for ages four to six. Conversely, a six-year-old who is still building vocabulary might enjoy a simpler picture book with rich illustrations. Always preview the text length and vocabulary yourself before deciding.

How do I know if a chapter book is the right reading level for my child?

Early reader chapter books usually land somewhere between a guided reading level J and M, which roughly corresponds to late first grade through third grade. The best way to gauge level is to have your child read a page aloud, if they stumble on more than three or four words on a typical page, the book is likely too hard for independent reading. Another practical method is the “five-finger rule”: hold up a finger for every unknown word on a page. One to two fingers means the book is probably a good fit; three suggests a challenge that may work with support; four or five signals frustration. Don’t overlook the importance of interest, a motivated child will tackle a slightly harder book if the topic excites them.

What should I look for in the illustrations of a picture book?

Illustrations in a picture book should do more than decorate the text: they should extend the story, show emotion, and give clues to the plot. Look for clear, expressive characters and a visual flow that matches the pacing of the story. In a well-designed picture book, a child can “read” the pictures to understand what’s happening even before they can decode the words. For younger children (ages 2 to 4), bold, uncluttered images with high contrast help hold attention. For older preschoolers, richer detail and subtle visual jokes reward repeated readings. Avoid illustrations that are so busy they overwhelm the page or so static they fail to convey action, those can confuse or bore a young listener.

How can I find books that match my child's interests and hobbies?

Start by paying attention to what your child already talks about, dinosaurs, trains, ballet, space, animals, or a favorite TV show. Many publishers have series that tap into popular subjects: for example, the “Fly Guy” series works well for kids who love humor and insects, while “Magic Tree House” appeals to history and adventure fans. Libraries often organize picture books and early readers by topic, so browsing those sections can spark ideas. If your child loves a particular author or illustrator, search for more works by that creator. Series books are especially powerful for early readers because familiarity reduces anxiety about vocabulary and plot. And don’t be afraid to mix nonfiction with fiction: a child who loves trucks might devour a photographic truck book alongside a story about a friendly tow truck.

Are there durability features I should look for in books for young children?

For toddlers and preschoolers, board books (thick, laminated cardboard pages) are the best choice, they survive drool, turning, and even chewing. Look for rounded corners to prevent pokes and a sturdy spine that won’t crack after a few reads. For slightly older kids who still manhandle books, a “paper-over-board” or “glued” binding is more durable than a traditional paper dust jacket. Paperbacks for early readers should have a reinforced binding or at least a thick cover. Avoid very thin, flimsy paperbacks that tear easily, some budget-friendly editions use lightweight paper that can’t withstand repeated backpack rides. If your child carries books to school or on trips, consider buying a few sturdy hardcovers for favorites; they cost more but often last through multiple children.

How can I tell if the language in an early reader chapter book is appropriate?

The best early reader chapter books use short sentences, limited vocabulary, and plenty of repetition to build reading confidence. Look for books labelled “I Can Read!” (levels 1–4) or “Step into Reading” (levels 1–5) that clearly indicate vocabulary range and sentence length. A level 1 book should have mostly one-syllable words and repeated sight words, while a level 3 might introduce compound sentences and a few challenging multisyllabic words. Read the first chapter aloud yourself. Does the sentence rhythm feel natural? Are there too many unfamiliar proper names? A good early reader paragraph typically has three to five sentences, and each sentence is no longer than about ten words. If the text feels choppy or the vocabulary seems arbitrary, the book may frustrate rather than encourage a new reader.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a picture book and an early reader chapter book?

A picture book relies heavily on illustrations to tell the story, with text that is generally intended to be read aloud to a child. An early reader chapter book has fewer illustrations per page and uses controlled vocabulary so a child can read it independently, often spanning short chapters of a few pages each.

How many words should a first chapter book have?

A typical early reader chapter book ranges from about 1,000 to 8,000 words total, with short chapters of 500 to 1,000 words each. The “Ivy and Bean” series or “Mercy Watson” are good examples, their word counts stay under 6,000, which is manageable for a beginning independent reader.

Are board books only for babies?

No, board books can be enjoyed by toddlers and even preschoolers who still treat books roughly. Many board books now feature simple stories, first concept learning (colors, shapes, letters), and sturdy pages that hold up to enthusiastic turning. They are an excellent choice for any child under age 3 or for busy families who want durable books on the go.

Should I only buy award-winning books for my child?

Awards like the Caldecott Medal or Newbery Medal signal high quality, but they don’t guarantee a particular child will love a book. Use award lists as a starting point to discover well-reviewed titles, then let your child’s reaction guide future purchases. Many fantastic books never win an award but are perfect for your family.

What is the five-finger rule for choosing a book?

The five-finger rule is a simple reading-level check: have your child read one page aloud and hold up a finger for every word they don’t know. Zero to one finger means the book is too easy; two to three fingers indicates a good challenge; four or more suggests the book is too hard for independent reading.

How do I handle a child who only wants to read the same book over and over?

Repetition is actually beneficial for early literacy, it builds vocabulary, comprehension, and confidence. The child is internalizing story structure and language patterns. Repeating a favorite while gradually offering one new book each week works well. The repeated book will lose its appeal naturally as the child’s skills grow.

In shortThe very best kid’s book is the one your child actually wants to read (or have read to them) again and again. Trust your instincts, lean into their passions, and remember that a book that’s a little too easy builds confidence while a book that’s a pinch too hard builds skills, when the interest is there. Happy reading.
